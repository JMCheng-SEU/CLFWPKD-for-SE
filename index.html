Residual Fusion Probabilistic Knowledge Distillation for Speech Enhancement

Jiaming Cheng, Ruiyu Liang, Lin Zhou, Li Zhao, Chengwei Huang, and Björn W. Schuller

Abstract

In recent years, there has been a great deal of research in developing neural network (NN)-based speech enhancement (SE) models, achieving promising results. However, NN-based models typically require expensive computational costs to show remarkable performance, constraining their deployment in real-world scenarios, especially when hardware resources are limited, or latency requirements are strict. To reduce this computational burden, we propose a residual fusion probabilistic knowledge distillation (KD) method for the SE task, transferring knowledge from a deep teacher to a shallower student model. Previous KD approaches commonly focus on narrowing the output distances between teachers and students, lacking research on the intermediate representation of the models. In this paper, we first study the cross-layer residual feature fusion strategy, enabling the student model to distill knowledge contained in multiple teacher layers from shallow to deep. Secondly, frame weighting probabilistic distillation loss is proposed to assign more emphasis on frames containing essential information and preserve pairwise probabilistic similarities in the representation space. The proposed distillation framework is applied to the dual-path dilated convolutional recurrent network (DPDCRN) that won the championship of the SE track in the L3DAS23 challenge. Extensive experiments are conducted on single-channel and multi-channel SE datasets, respectively. Objective evaluations show that the proposed KD strategy outperforms other distillation methods and considerably improves the enhancement effect of the low-complexity student model (with only 17% of the teacher's parameters).

2. L3DAS23 Dataset

L3DAS23 task 1: 3D speech enhancement. The target of the task is to extract the monophonic signal from the 3D mixture containing various background noises. The speech data contains over 4000 virtual 3D audio for up to 12 seconds. The noise set selects 12 transient noise classes that can be heard in an office and 4 continuous noise classes. For the challenge, two Ambisonics microphones are placed in 443 random positions of 68 houses, and B-Format ACN/SN3D impulse responses of the rooms are generated by placing the sound sources in random locations of a cylindrical grid. The speech data are released as 8-channel 16kHz 16-bit WAV files, including two first-order Ambisonics recordings (each with 4 channels).
